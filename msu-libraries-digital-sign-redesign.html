MSU Libraries
Digital Signs

Research Study and Redesign

Project Overview

During Fall of 2023, I worked together with Michigan State University Libraries to redesign their digital library navigation signs. These signs are placed in large touchscreens around the library. They were looking to increase the usability of their signs in order to encourage more library patrons to utilize the signs.

My Contributions

With a group of three other team members, we performed a user research study to find what problems users were running into while using the signs. Using the information gained from the study, My team and I redesigned the signs to improve usibility, and created a working prototype in Figma.

The original design's home page and "Find A Location" page

Project Background

The Michigan State University Libraries originally created interactive and digital signs placed around their library in order to provide MSUÂ students, staff, and community members a quick and efficient way to find books and information about the MSU library while they visit. The digital signs allowed for a sustainable and efficent way to provide this information. These signs are placed on large touchscreens throughout the library.

Other sign pages

After meeting with a representative from the MSU Libraries, we learned that they were looking for an improved design of the signs, focusing on these 5 values:

  • Accessibility
  • Usability
  • Sustainablity (in terms of materials and labor)
  • Intuitive design
  • Eye-catching but not distracting design

Taking these values into account, we decided to first do a research analysis study on the current use of the signs. This included conducting a field study and user interviews at the MSU Library, creating user personas, and doing a heuristic analysis on the current design before starting to wireframe and create prototypes.

Field Study

To find library patrons to perform these tasks, we went to the MSU Main Library and asked three random people there if they were willing to participate in our research study. We specifically looked for one Michigan State University student, one library employee, and one non-student visitor in order to get opinions from a wide range of people. We did not want to solely ask MSUÂ students in order to prevent bias in our research study.

After conducting the study, we found the following common pain points:Â 

  • Trouble clicking options. It was not accessible to people in wheelchairs, or those with low dexterity/hand strength
  • The maps did not change obviously enough to the user
  • Lack additional information provided, especially for library events
  • Everyone wanted a search bar to improve ease of finding things

User Personas

After conducting our field research study, we were able to use those three participants to create three different personas, representing three types of people who might interact with the digital signs at the MSU Main Library. This included one student, one librarian, and one elderly visitor.

User personas and scenarios

In addition to the three user persona profiles that we created, we attached corresponding scenarios to go along with them. These scenarios are a small story that futher describes who each persona is, their background, and how they would interact with the digital library signs.

These personas and scenarios allowed for my team and I to gain better insight into the needs, goals, and behaviors of the target users for the MSUÂ digital library signs. This acted as a reference for creating the redesign, as well as improving usability and accessibility.

Heuristic Analysis

As a last step before starting the redesign process, we conducted a heuristic evaluation and analysis on the current signs. We decided to look at four different pages on the app, allowing us to save time yet still have a good grasp on the usability of the signs as a whole. This allowed us to test and evaluate the current usability of the signs based on standard criteria. So, taking a look at Home, Find A Book, Events, and Maps pages, we evaluated them using the follwing criteria:

  • Visibility of system status
  • Match between the system and real world
  • User control and freedom
  • Consistency and standards
  • Recognition rather than recall
  • Flexibility and efficiency of use
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design
  • Help and documentation

Using this criteria we noticed the lower green icons, listed events, and library hours displayed are not interactive on the home page. Also on the home page, there was inconsistent font sizes and spacing, as well as a lot of white space and no option for customization or accessibility.

Looking at the find a book page, I noticed that the design seemed cramped and the layout, colors, links, and margins were inconsistent with other pages. The maps page also had the same issue. In addition, we saw that the call number system may be confusing, especially if you do not have your call number on hand. The maps page also had location titles that may be confusing to unfamiliar patrons.

Lastly, the events page had vague event titles with no additional information details, and lengthy titles were cut off without an option for expansion. I also noticed there was no user control nor interaction features provided. In addition, I saw that layout of this page also needed consistent sizing and contrast.

Design Process

Taking all of this research and information into account, my team and I started off by creating a few simple low-fidelity wireframes to share with each other. This way we would have multiple options to think over and choose from. We made these in Google Slides because the size of the screens the signs would be placed one are nearly the same dimension ratio as a default Google Slide. We used different shapes and rectangles to represent objects in the wireframes.

Some of our low-fidelity home page ideas

After each of my team members created their ideas for each page, we grouped together to discuss our designs, make adjustments and edits, and choose which wireframe layouts we wanted to use for the final protototype. However, during our meeting we took a look at the hardware capablities of the screens the signs would be placed on and realized that only the lower 1/3 of the screen had touchscreen capabilities. Because of this, some wireframes were unusable.

Below are Â the low fidelity wireframes that we chose to proceed with.

Low fidelity wireframes

After the wireframes were chosen, we recreated them in Figma and transformed them into medium fidelity wireframes. These designs provided multiple solutions to some of the accessibility and usability problems that were highlighted in the field study, user personas, and heuristic analysis. For instance, the events page now allows users to view additional information about each event, a button to chat with a librarian was added to let patrons quickly ask questions, maps are expandable and easier to read, and navigation menu items are now bolded and highlighted so users can easily identify which option they are currently on. In addition, all of the pages use a grid design system in order to make sure that the layout and visual design is consistent across all pages.

Once we had the wireframes in Figma, we were able to create a working high-fidelity prototype that emphasizes accessability and interactivity, while remaining low maintenance in terms of software limitations.

The final Figma prototype

Reflection

Looking back, I would love to have edited our final Figma prototype so that the event's page also had a menu bar at the bottom, to keep consistent with other pages. I also would have liked to add more people to our field study if I were to do this again. This way, we would have more insight into what a wide variety of library patrons think and feel about the signs. In addition, if I were to further this project, I would do some user testing and research on the new prototype that my team and I created, in order to further tweak the design and make sure that it was user friendly and accessible.

However, this research study and redesign project has been a great learning opportunity that has taught me more about how to conduct user research and use it in order to create a good design. This opportunity was also a huge success in terms of teamwork. My team and I divided up our roles , and set deadlines for each other to keep each other on track, and this led to great teamwork skills and increased productivity for all of us. It showed me that when you form good relationships with your team, it leads to better design!
